The Islamic science of the Akhir al-Zaman — the End of Times — has been systematically misrepresented in both Western academic discourse and in popular Muslim religiosity as mythology, prophecy, or theological speculation. This monograph applies it as something else entirely: as formal structural metaphysics compiled in the tradition of the Safavid seminaries, integrated with contemporary critical theory and geopolitical analysis to produce what is, in the SCRA's assessment, the most precise available framework for understanding civilizational transition dynamics. The claim is not that the eschatological prophecies are literally predictive. The claim is that the structural logic encoded in the prophetic tradition — about how power concentrates, how systems become maximally fragile at the moment of maximum apparent strength, and how the authentic transmission reasserts itself through the very pressure that attempts to suppress it — constitutes a genuine science of civilizational dynamics that the modern social sciences, for structural reasons, cannot fully articulate.

The central instrument is what this monograph calls the Ghaybah Engine — a conceptual apparatus built on Mulla Sadra's doctrine of al-Harakat al-Jawhariyyah (Substantial Motion) applied to the phenomenon of the Major Occultation (the withdrawal of the Twelfth Imam from public presence in 941 CE). Sadra's doctrine holds that being itself is in continuous motion — not merely objects moving through static being, but the very substance of existence undergoing continuous intensification toward its divine source. Applied to the Ghaybah (Occultation): the apparent absence of the Imam is not a reduction but an intensification. The withdrawal of the custodial presence from the visible surface produces a compression — the authentic transmission concentrates into increasingly non-institutional forms (the silsila, the dargah, the underground chain) while the institutional surface becomes progressively more captured and therefore more fragile. Complex systems become maximally fragile at the precise moment of maximum apparent power. The monograph reads the present geopolitical configuration through this lens.

Ten Chapters · Four Internal Volumes

  • VOL. I · CH. I–II · THE COMPRESSION · Chapter I — The Ghaybah Engine: the technical apparatus for understanding the Major Occultation as a civilizational dynamic rather than a purely theological event. Mulla Sadra's al-Harakat al-Jawhariyyah (Substantial Motion) establishes that being does not exist in static states — substance itself is in continuous motion, intensifying toward its source. The Ghaybah Engine applies this doctrine to the eschatological situation: the withdrawal of the living custodian from the visible surface does not reduce the transmission's intensity — it concentrates it. Just as water compressed into a smaller vessel increases pressure, the authentic transmission compressed into non-institutional forms (the Sufi silsila, the inner circle of scholars, the private chains of teaching) acquires intensity rather than diffusing. The Compression Phase is the period from the beginning of the Major Occultation (941 CE) to the point of maximum compression — the point at which the institutional surface is maximally captured, the underground chain is maximally concentrated, and the system is approaching the threshold of reconfiguration. The geopolitical indicators of maximum compression: centralization of global power into a single hegemonic system, the elimination of all alternative epistemic authorities, the complete capture of all institutional religious and educational structures by the Ba'alist apparatus. Chapter II — Substantial Motion as Eschatological Ontology: the philosophical argument for why the Sadrian doctrine of Substantial Motion is the correct ontological framework for eschatological analysis. The key claim: eschatology is not about the end of time but about the full actualization of what time has always been moving toward. The Zuhur (the return of the custodial presence) is not a miraculous interruption of natural order — it is the full emergence of what Substantial Motion has been producing throughout the compression phase. The monograph documents the specific Sadrian texts (al-Asfar al-Arba'a, al-Shawahid al-Rububiyyah, al-Mabda wa al-Ma'ad) in which the eschatological reading of Substantial Motion is most explicitly developed, and traces the transmission of this reading from Mulla Sadra through his student Fayz Kashani, through Mulla Hadi Sabzevari, into the contemporary Qom-Mashhad philosophical tradition.
  • VOL. II · CH. III–IV · SEMIOTICS OF THE END · Chapter III — Black Banners as Batini Inheritance Code: the prophetic tradition's repeated reference to Black Banners from Khorasan as a sign of the Mahdi's emergence is not a physical description but a semiotic code — a reference to the batini (interior, esoteric) inheritance of the Khorasan transmission chain. The Black Banner in the prophetic tradition is associated with the Abbasid revolution (750 CE), which used black as its revolutionary color and Khorasan as its base of operations — but the prophetic tradition locates the authentic Black Banners in the Akhir al-Zaman, implying that the Khorasan transmission chain will produce a second emergence that fulfills what the Abbasid appropriation claimed but did not deliver. The batini reading: the Black Banners are not a military formation but the surface marker of the underground chain surfacing — the Khorasan transmission chain, having preserved the authentic synthesis through the closure architecture, emerging into public visibility at the moment of maximum civilizational pressure. Chapter IV — Taleqan as Eschatological Node: the prophetic tradition specifically names Taleqan (a region in what is now northeastern Afghanistan / northwestern Iran, in the heart of Greater Khorasan) as a node of the eschatological transmission. Taleqan is not a major city or political center — its significance is geographic and ontological: it is in the Khorasan corridor, at the eastern edge of the Persian civilizational sphere, at the precise geographic overlap between the Safavid philosophical tradition and the Central Asian Sufi transmission networks. The geographic ontology of Greater Khorasan as the monograph maps it: not a modern nation-state but a transmission zone stretching from eastern Iran through Afghanistan, through the northern Indus corridor, encompassing the primary nodes of the Sufi transmission chain (Mashhad, Herat, Kabul, Lahore, Ajmer). The SCRA's geographic thesis: the Khorasan corridor is not defined by modern political boundaries but by the presence of the transmission chain's institutional nodes.
  • VOL. III · CH. V–VII · APPLIED GEOPOLITICAL ANALYSIS · Chapter V — The Iranian Axis: the Islamic Republic of Iran as a partial, imperfect, but structurally significant instantiation of the custodial prototype — the attempt to create a political order grounded in the metaphysical framework of the Khorasan transmission rather than in secular nationalism or colonial-era institutional frameworks. The doctrine of Wilayat al-Faqih (Guardianship of the Jurist) as the post-Occultation institutional attempt to maintain custodial governance in the absence of the Imam: not the authentic transmission itself, but the closest institutional approximation available in the compression phase. The monograph neither celebrates nor dismisses the Islamic Republic — it analyzes it structurally: what does it represent in terms of the transmission chain's attempt to maintain political expression during the compression phase? What are its systemic vulnerabilities to the same Ba'alist capture mechanism that destroyed every previous institutional expression? Chapter VI — The Resistance Formations: Hezbollah (Lebanon), Hamas (Palestine), the Houthi movement (Yemen), and the Iraqi Resistance as non-state formations that operate outside the Ba'alist capture mechanism precisely because they lack the institutional address required for capture. The structural analysis: the Resistance formations are not primarily military organizations. They are organizational expressions of the underground chain's political emergence — communities that have been pushed outside the institutional apparatus of the modern nation-state by the Ba'alist compression and have therefore developed non-institutional forms of social organization that replicate, in the political domain, what the Sufi silsila represents in the epistemic domain. Chapter VII — The Dajjal Principle: the prophetic figure of the Dajjal (the Great Deceiver, the Antichrist in Islamic eschatology) analyzed not as a future individual but as a structural principle — the principle of maximum apparent power combined with maximum falsification of the real. The Dajjal Principle is the Ba'alist capture mechanism operating at civilizational scale: the most powerful system presents itself as the bearer of truth, justice, and civilization while systematically producing falsehood, injustice, and civilizational destruction. The monograph's application: the unipolar global order that emerged after 1991 — with its claim to represent universal values, human rights, and civilizational progress while operating the most extensive apparatus of extraction, surveillance, and military violence in human history — is the most complete historical instantiation of the Dajjal Principle available for structural analysis.
  • VOL. IV · CH. VIII–X · ETHICS OF THE THRESHOLD · Chapter VIII — Ethics of the Threshold: what does the compression-phase eschatological framework require of those who understand it? The monograph identifies three ethical postures available to someone who recognizes the Ghaybah Engine as operational. The posture of Sabr (patient endurance): maintaining transmission in the compression phase without the expectation of immediate political emergence. The posture of Istidad (preparation): building the non-institutional infrastructure — knowledge transmission, community formation, economic independence from the captured apparatus — that the Zuhur Sequence will require. The posture of Tawakkul (complete reliance on the divine): the recognition that the Compression Phase is not a failure of the transmission but its most intense form, and that the human obligation is not to force the emergence but to remain faithful to the chain. Chapter IX — The Zuhur Sequence: the prophetic tradition's account of the signs preceding and accompanying the Zuhur (the return/emergence of the Mahdi) analyzed as a structural sequence rather than a literal prediction. The Zuhur Sequence in structural terms: (1) maximum civilizational compression — the Ba'alist apparatus reaches its greatest extent; (2) the Sufyani event — a catastrophic assertion of false custodianship that accelerates the collapse of the compressed system; (3) the Nida (the Divine Call) — the semiotic event that signals the reconfiguration; (4) the emergence of the custodial presence into public visibility; (5) the rapid restructuring of the civilizational configuration around the re-emergent authentic transmission. The monograph reads the current geopolitical configuration against this sequence and identifies specific structural indicators of where in the sequence the present moment falls. Chapter X — The Raj'ah, the Abrahamic Convergence, and the Post-Zuhur World: the Raj'ah (the Return — the concept in Shia eschatology of the return of specific souls to complete unfinished civilizational work) as not a supernatural assertion but a structural claim about the continuity of transmission across apparent historical rupture. The Abrahamic convergence: the eschatological tradition's placement of Isa ibn Maryam (Jesus) within the Zuhur Sequence as a figure of Abrahamic convergence — not the Christian Second Coming, not a Muslim assertion of Islamic supersession, but the prophetic tradition's acknowledgment that the authentic transmission chain of the Abrahamic lineage is one chain that will converge at the point of maximum civilizational reconfiguration. The post-Zuhur world as what the entire compression phase has been moving toward: the actualization of the Sadrian Substantial Motion's telos — being fully expressing its inherent orientation toward its divine source, the authentic transmission no longer underground but the organizing principle of public life.
