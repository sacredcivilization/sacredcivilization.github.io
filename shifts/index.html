Ba'l — The Semitic Claim to Lordship

ETYMOLOGY & QURANIC PRIMARY SOURCE

Ba'l (بَعْل) derives from the Semitic root BʿL — meaning "lord," "owner," "master." The same root produces Hebrew ba'al, Aramaic be'el, Akkadian belu. In ancient Semitic religious practice the title was applied to any deity claiming sovereignty over a domain: Baal Hadad (lord of storms), Baal Hammon (lord of the furnace), Baal Shamin (lord of heaven).

Quran 37:125 — Allah addresses the community of the Prophet Ilyas (Elijah): "Do you call upon Ba'l and abandon the Best of Creators?" Ibn Kathir (Tafsir): Ba'l here is the idol worshipped by Bani Israel — the Quran's indictment is not of a single deity but of the structural act of substituting a false lordship-claim for divine authority. Al-Tabari records that the name Ba'l was understood by the Arabs as denoting any false claimant to Rububiyyah (lordship over creation) — a category, not a proper name. The Quran names the category, not just one instance.
