The standard historiography of Islamic intellectual decline locates the rupture in the Mongol invasion of 1258 CE or in the alleged internal exhaustion of Islamic civilization. This monograph refutes both accounts with a more precise and more disturbing thesis: the authentic transmission chain was not destroyed from outside — it was progressively captured from within, by the political states that claimed to represent Islam while systematically severing the population from the living custodial tradition that the Prophet's household had transmitted.

The instrument of this capture is what this archive calls the Ba'alist mechanism: the absorption of the authentic tradition's language, institutional authority, and cultural prestige — combined with the simultaneous elimination of the living transmitters who held the source. The Golden Age was real. So was the theft. Both happened at the same time, in the same institution, under the same caliphal dynasty. This monograph documents the full four-stage architecture of the closure and the underground chain that survived every stage of it.

Six Chapters

  • Chapter I. The Ba'alist Capture Pattern — the structural thesis: how every stage of the closure followed the same logic — separate the knowledge from its custodial chain, then declare the chain either illegitimate or unnecessary; why this pattern recurs across fourteen centuries; the Quranic ontological framework (Haq/Batil, admixture doctrine) as the theoretical lens
  • Chapter II. The First Capture — Saqifa (632 CE) and the Abbasid Extraction (750–850 CE): the political separation of the Prophetic House from state power; the Abbasid caliphate's simultaneous construction of the House of Wisdom from the intellectual legacy of Imam al-Sadiq's school and imprisonment of the Imams who held the living source; the extraction-without-attribution pattern as the template for all subsequent closures
  • Chapter III. The Khawarij Protocol — text without transmission: the Battle of Siffin (657 CE) as the first formulation of the epistemological break; the claim that the Quran speaks directly without the mediation of the transmitting Imam; why this is structurally identical to the Wahhabi and Salafi epistemological position twelve centuries later; the isnad severed from the matn
  • Chapter IV. Ibn Taymiyyah's Jurisprudential Walls — three closure mechanisms: (i) the declared closure of ijtihad, (ii) the fatwa declaring the Sufi silsila chains as bid'ah and shirk, (iii) the sealed-room ontology that excluded Ishraqi metaphysics from the boundaries of legitimate Islamic discourse; why Ibn Taymiyyah was the architectural genius of the closure — not the originator of its premise but the builder of its permanent institutional infrastructure
  • Chapter V. The Underground Chain — how the authentic transmission survived the closure architecture: the Sufi silsila as the non-state transmission vehicle; the dargah as the institutional node of the Khorasan-Indus corridor; Hujwiri in Lahore (11th c.), the Chishti network across the Indus basin, the Safavid philosophical school (Mulla Sadra), and Iqbal as the moment when the underground chain speaks back into the present without reference to any state's validation
  • Chapter VI. The Wahhabi-Deobandi Institutional Seal — the 18th–19th century completion of the closure architecture: Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab's weaponization of Ibn Taymiyyah against the living dargah networks of the Arabian Peninsula; the Deoband madrasa system (est. 1867) as the colonial-era institutionalization of the Taimiyyan closure against the Khorasan-Indus transmission chain; the Dars-i-Nizami curriculum as the replacement of the living silsila with the printed credential; the Barelvi counter as the underground chain's direct institutional response
